# Compliance Requirements Reference
> Common compliance items for residential real estate transactions in the United States. State-specific requirements vary — verify all items against your state's real estate commission rules and your brokerage's compliance policy.

---

## Required Disclosures by Transaction Type

### Residential Purchase — All States
These disclosures are federally mandated or near-universally required across all states.

| Disclosure | Requirement | Trigger |
|---|---|---|
| Lead-Based Paint Disclosure | Federal — 42 U.S.C. 4852d | All homes built before January 1, 1978 |
| Lead-Based Paint Pamphlet | Federal — HUD/EPA required | Delivered alongside lead disclosure |
| Agency Disclosure | State — virtually universal | Before writing or presenting any offer |
| Loan Estimate (LE) | Federal — TRID (12 CFR 1026.19) | Within 3 business days of loan application |
| Closing Disclosure (CD) | Federal — TRID (12 CFR 1026.19) | Minimum 3 business days before consummation |

### Seller's Property Disclosure
Required in most states. The seller must disclose known material defects. The scope varies significantly:

- **Full Disclosure States** (e.g., California, Washington): Comprehensive form covering structural, mechanical, environmental, legal, and neighborhood conditions
- **Caveat Emptor States** (e.g., Georgia for commercial): Buyer beware — limited seller disclosure obligation; however, agents must still disclose known material defects
- **Hybrid States**: Basic disclosure required with specific items enumerated by statute

Common required disclosure items (where applicable by state):
- [ ] Roof condition and age
- [ ] Water intrusion or flooding history
- [ ] Foundation issues — past or present
- [ ] HVAC system age and known defects
- [ ] Pest/termite history and treatments
- [ ] Environmental hazards: radon, asbestos, lead paint, mold, underground storage tanks
- [ ] Boundary disputes or encroachments
- [ ] Zoning violations or unpermitted work
- [ ] HOA special assessments pending or approved
- [ ] Neighborhood nuisances: noise, odor, flight paths
- [ ] Prior insurance claims on the property
- [ ] Death on the property (disclosure requirements vary by state — California requires for deaths within 3 years; some states have no requirement)
- [ ] Methamphetamine manufacturing history (required in several states)

### New Construction
- [ ] Builder's warranty terms (typically 1-2-10: 1 year workmanship, 2 years systems, 10 years structural)
- [ ] Final certificate of occupancy
- [ ] Energy efficiency disclosures (required in some states)
- [ ] Soil report / geological report (in earthquake zones or expansive soil areas)

### Condo / Townhome / HOA Transactions
- [ ] HOA financial statements (current year budget and reserve study)
- [ ] HOA meeting minutes (typically 12-24 months)
- [ ] CC&Rs and bylaws
- [ ] HOA rules and regulations
- [ ] Pending litigation disclosure
- [ ] Special assessments — approved or pending
- [ ] Rental restrictions
- [ ] Pet restrictions
- [ ] Resale certificate / estoppel letter from HOA

### Short Sale Transactions
- [ ] Short sale addendum — terms of lender approval
- [ ] Listing agent short sale authorization from seller
- [ ] Lender approval letter — must be received before removal of short sale contingency
- [ ] Deficiency waiver documentation (state-specific)

### REO / Bank-Owned Transactions
- [ ] As-is addendum
- [ ] Bank's disclosure addendum (supersedes standard seller disclosure in most cases)
- [ ] Property condition report (if provided by asset manager)
- [ ] Occupancy status confirmation (tenant-occupied REO — federal protections apply)

## Wire Fraud Prevention Checklist

Wire fraud targeting real estate transactions is the most common type of cybercrime in the industry, costing buyers and sellers hundreds of millions of dollars annually. Every transaction file must include evidence that the following protocols were followed.

### Agent Responsibilities
- [ ] Educate buyer and seller about wire fraud at the first meeting — document that the conversation occurred
- [ ] Never send wire instructions by email
- [ ] Never forward wire instructions received by email without verbal verification
- [ ] Confirm all wire instructions by calling the title company at a phone number obtained independently (not from an email)
- [ ] Document the verbal confirmation: date, time, person spoken to, confirmation number
- [ ] Instruct clients never to wire funds based on email instructions alone

### Buyer Responsibilities (as communicated by agent)
- [ ] Call title company directly on a known number to confirm wire instructions before sending any funds
- [ ] Verify routing number and account number verbally — read back to title officer
- [ ] Confirm wire receipt with title company after sending
- [ ] Be suspicious of any last-minute changes to wire instructions — this is the most common fraud vector

### Title/Escrow Responsibilities (verify with your title company)
- [ ] Written wire fraud warning included in initial escrow instructions
- [ ] Encrypted portal used for wire instruction delivery where available
- [ ] Title company confirms receipt of incoming wires and notifies buyer's agent

### SkySlope Documentation
- [ ] Wire fraud prevention disclosure uploaded to file
- [ ] Agent note documenting verbal confirmation of wire instructions (date, time, title contact)

## Fair Housing Compliance Notes

All real estate transactions must comply with the Fair Housing Act (42 U.S.C. Chapter 45) and applicable state and local fair housing laws.

### Protected Classes — Federal
1. Race
2. Color
3. Religion
4. National Origin
5. Sex (includes gender identity and sexual orientation — HUD guidance)
6. Disability
7. Familial Status (families with children under 18, pregnant women)

### Additional Protected Classes — Common State and Local Additions
- Marital status
- Age
- Source of income (including Section 8 vouchers — several major jurisdictions)
- Sexual orientation and gender identity (where not federally covered)
- Military or veteran status
- Citizenship status

### Agent Compliance in Transactions
- [ ] Never steer buyers toward or away from neighborhoods based on protected class characteristics
- [ ] All offers presented to seller regardless of buyer characteristics
- [ ] Marketing uses inclusive language and images
- [ ] Reasonable accommodation requests from buyers or sellers with disabilities documented and handled appropriately
- [ ] Any accommodation requests for inspection or access addressed without discrimination

### SkySlope File Requirements
- [ ] No notes or communications in the file containing protected class references related to buyer or seller
- [ ] All offers documented — dual agency files must show all offers presented to seller

## Document Retention Requirements

### Federal Baseline
The IRS requires real estate professionals to retain records for a minimum of 3 years from the date the transaction closed. However, the industry standard and most state requirements are significantly longer.

### State Requirements (verify for your state)
Most states require 3-7 years. Common standards:

| State | Minimum Retention | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| California | 3 years | DRE requirement from close of transaction |
| Texas | 4 years | TREC requirement |
| Florida | 5 years | FREC requirement |
| New York | 3 years | DOS requirement |
| Washington | 3 years | DOL requirement |

**Best practice: Retain all transaction files for 7 years.** This exceeds most state requirements and covers the typical statute of limitations for real estate fraud claims.

### What Must Be Retained
- [ ] Executed Purchase and Sale Agreement with all addenda
- [ ] Agency disclosure forms
- [ ] Seller's property disclosure statement
- [ ] All offers submitted (accepted, rejected, and countered)
- [ ] Earnest money deposit records
- [ ] Escrow and title documents
- [ ] Closing Disclosure / HUD-1 Settlement Statement
- [ ] All inspection reports and responses
- [ ] Correspondence with all parties (email, text logs if transactional)
- [ ] MLS listing documents
- [ ] All marketing materials
- [ ] Transaction coordinator agreements

### SkySlope Retention
SkySlope retains closed and archived transaction files within the platform. Brokerages should confirm:
- [ ] SkySlope subscription terms include multi-year file retention
- [ ] Brokerage has a backup export policy for SkySlope files in case of platform change
- [ ] Access to closed files is restricted to authorized users only

## RESPA Compliance Notes

The Real Estate Settlement Procedures Act (RESPA — 12 U.S.C. 2601 et seq.) governs federally related mortgage transactions.

### Section 8 — Kickbacks and Referral Fees
- No referral fees between settlement service providers unless disclosed and compliant
- Affiliated Business Arrangements (AfBA) must be disclosed using the HUD-approved AfBA Disclosure form
- No fee-splitting for services not actually performed

### Section 9 — Title Insurance
- Sellers cannot require buyers to use a specific title insurance company as a condition of sale

### Section 10 — Escrow Accounts
- Lenders cannot collect excessive escrow cushions
- Maximum cushion: 2 months of monthly escrow payment

### TRID Compliance Summary (Know Before You Owe)
| Requirement | Rule | Timing |
|---|---|---|
| Loan Estimate delivery | 12 CFR 1026.19(e) | Within 3 business days of application |
| Loan Estimate expiration | 12 CFR 1026.19(e)(3)(iv) | 10 business days from delivery |
| Closing Disclosure delivery | 12 CFR 1026.19(f) | Minimum 3 business days before consummation |
| Revised CD — increased APR | 12 CFR 1026.19(f)(2)(ii) | Restarts 3 business day waiting period |
| Revised CD — loan product change | 12 CFR 1026.19(f)(2)(ii) | Restarts 3 business day waiting period |

**Note**: TRID "business days" for the Closing Disclosure are all calendar days except Sundays and federal public holidays. "Business days" for the Loan Estimate are days on which the creditor's offices are open to the public. These are different definitions — confirm with the lender.
